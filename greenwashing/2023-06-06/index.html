Companies Embrace Sustainability While Others Engage in Deceptive Practices

Published: 2023-06-06

Covia, a company focused on environmental stewardship, recently hosted a virtual event to celebrate World Environment Day. The event aimed to inspire team members to support environmental goals and efforts, particularly in the fight against plastic pollution. Covia’s environmental initiatives are aimed at improving air quality, wildlife habitat, and other important environmental factors in the communities they operate in.

However, not all companies are as transparent and committed to sustainability as Covia. Some engage in deceptive practices known as greenwashing, where they misrepresent their environmental efforts to appear more eco-friendly than they actually are.

One recent example of greenwashing comes from Shell, a company that promotes its low-carbon products but fails to mention its significant investment in oil and gas. The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) has banned three of Shell’s ads for misleadingly omitting information about the proportion of its business that is made up of lower-carbon activity. This kind of false advertising can mislead consumers and hinder the transition to renewable energy.

Another company taking steps towards sustainability is Japanese insurance company Nippon Life. They are increasing their efforts to promote sustainability among the companies in their portfolio. Nippon Life plans to engage in dialogue with these companies to track progress towards sustainability targets and escalate issues if progress is not made. This approach aligns with a global trend among investors to focus on sustainability outcomes rather than just managing ESG risks.

However, not all investors and corporations prioritize sustainability. Target, for example, has faced backlash for selling LGBT-themed items and clothing. Some investors see this as straying too far from the company’s core function of making money for shareholders. Tesla CEO Elon Musk even predicts lawsuits against Target for “destruction of shareholder value.” Despite the criticism, Target remains committed to supporting the LGBTQIA+ community.

Democratic Rep. Cori Bush has accused Republicans of endangering black and brown communities by opposing the use of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors in investment decisions. She argues that corporations that disregard the impact of pollution and climate change on marginalized communities are putting the burden on those communities. ESG, also known as sustainable investing, considers factors such as gun control, environmental issues, and abortion in addition to profitability.

On the other hand, companies like Mastercard are making significant progress in advancing their environmental, social, and governance goals. Mastercard has closed the global pay gap between male and female employees, invested in Black communities, and engaged millions of girls through its Girls4Tech STEM program. They aim to build a sustainable economy and contribute to a more equitable and prosperous world.

Despite these positive efforts, investors in ESG equity funds became net sellers in May, seeking safe havens during market volatility. The sector has underperformed due to its exclusion of oil and gas companies, which have seen share prices rise during the energy crisis. Additionally, a survey found that consumers are prioritizing affordability over sustainability, with the number of people making planet-friendly decisions nearly halving.

In conclusion, while some companies like Covia and Mastercard are genuinely committed to sustainability, others engage in deceptive practices like greenwashing. It is important for consumers and investors to be aware of these tactics and hold companies accountable for their actions. By supporting companies that prioritize sustainability and transparency, we can work towards a better and greener future for all.
