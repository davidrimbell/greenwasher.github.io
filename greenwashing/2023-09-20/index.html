The Battle Against Greenwashing: Governments and Organizations Demand Greater Transparency and Accountability

Published: 2023-09-20

The fight against greenwashing is gaining momentum as governments, organizations, and consumers demand greater transparency and accountability from companies. Greenwashing, which refers to the deceptive practice of making false or exaggerated claims about the environmental benefits of products or services, has become a growing concern in recent years. With the increasing urgency of the climate crisis, it is more important than ever to ensure that companies are not misleading consumers with false advertising and deceitful marketing tactics.

One significant development in the battle against greenwashing is the proposed law by the European Commission to ban companies from making climate-neutral claims without substantiating them. This law, set to be implemented from 2026, aims to eliminate greenwashing and ensure that environmental claims are backed by proof of recognized performance. It will also prohibit claims based on emissions offsetting, which can often be misleading. The goal is to make products fit for a Europe heading towards climate neutrality and to ensure that companies are held accountable for their environmental claims.

The United Nations Secretary-General, António Guterres, has also spoken out against greenwashing, criticizing wealthy countries and the fossil fuel industry for their slow response to the climate crisis. Guterres called for wealthy countries to reach net-zero emissions by 2040 and deliver promised climate funding to poorer nations. While the recent climate summit attended by leaders from over 100 countries was a step in the right direction, Guterres acknowledged that it alone would not significantly alter the trajectory of the climate crisis. It is clear that more action is needed to combat greenwashing and address the urgent need for climate action.

The European Union (EU) is taking a strong stance against greenwashing by planning to ban vague environmental claims on consumer product packaging. The new regulations, set to take effect by 2026, will make the EU’s stance on greenwashing the strictest in the world. Only companies with proven “excellent” environmental credentials will be allowed to make such claims. This crackdown on greenwashing is part of the EU’s broader environmental policy to reduce greenhouse gas emissions to zero by 2050. By holding companies accountable for their environmental claims, the EU aims to prevent them from exploiting climate-conscious consumers.

In the financial sector, the International Federation of Accountants (IFAC) has found that more companies worldwide are engaging in sustainability reporting and receiving assurance services. However, the report also highlights that assurance rates have increased but cover a narrower set of topics. This lack of transparency raises concerns about potential greenwashing and the need for clearer guidelines and engagement with local stakeholders. Greater transparency and reporting on outcomes are crucial for companies to demonstrate their commitment to genuine sustainability practices.

The fight against greenwashing is not limited to regulations and reporting. It also involves promoting sustainable practices and innovation in various industries. For example, the construction industry is embracing cross-laminated timber (CLT) buildings, which offer durability, carbon sequestration, and a natural aesthetic. The success of projects like 1 De Haro in San Francisco paves the way for future CLT projects and promotes sustainability in the built environment.

In the investment sector, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is cracking down on deceptive marketing practices by investment funds in the United States. The new “Name Rule” requires that 80% of a fund’s portfolio matches the asset advertised by its name. This rule aims to address the issue of greenwashing and misleading investors who are interested in environmental, social, and governance (ESG) investing. By holding funds accountable for their claims, the SEC aims to protect investors and promote transparency in the industry.

Overall, the fight against greenwashing is gaining traction as governments, organizations, and consumers demand greater transparency and accountability. From proposed laws to ban misleading claims to increased reporting and assurance services, efforts are being made to ensure that companies are held accountable for their environmental claims. By combating greenwashing, we can create a more sustainable future and leave a better world for future generations.
