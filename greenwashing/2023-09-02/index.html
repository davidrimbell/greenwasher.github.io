Scientists Challenge Climate Emergency Declaration; Mamas & Papas Promotes Sustainability with Loved for Life Program; Tommy Diacono Protests at Burning Man Festival; North Dakota Faces Challenges from ESG Movement; Carbon Credits and the Potential for a Free Market Approach; KiwiSaver Funds Invested in Harmful Companies Reach Record High; Shareholder Derivative Lawsuits in Europe Aim for Corporate Commitment to Sustainability.

Published: 2023-09-02

A group of over 1,600 scientists, including two Nobel Prize winners, have signed a declaration challenging the idea of a climate emergency. Organized by the Climate Intelligence Foundation (CLINTEL), the declaration calls for climate science to be less political and for politicians to consider the real costs and benefits of their policy measures. The document challenges popular narratives about climate change, stating that the planet is warming slower than predicted and that natural disasters are actually on the decline. It also emphasizes the importance of carbon dioxide for life on Earth and promotes reforestation efforts.

While this declaration may seem controversial, it highlights the ongoing debate surrounding climate change and the need for a balanced and informed discussion. It is important to consider different perspectives and engage in open dialogue to find the best solutions for our planet.

In other news, nursery equipment specialist Mamas & Papas is taking steps towards sustainability with its Loved for Life program. The company is now selling refurbished second-hand pushchairs alongside new models in its Gateshead store. These pushchairs undergo a thorough safety check, repairs if necessary, and a professional deep clean before being sold with a six-month guarantee. This initiative encourages parents to reuse, refresh, or recycle baby items, reducing waste and promoting a circular economy. Mamas & Papas is also offering a Valet & Service option to extend the life of pushchairs. In September, proceeds from the sale of pre-loved pushchairs will be donated to Oxfam. These sustainability initiatives are part of Mamas & Papas' broader environmental, social, and governance (ESG) strategy.

On the topic of climate activism, Tommy Diacono, known for his fast-food brand New York Best, has turned his attention to climate change. Diacono protested at the Burning Man festival in Nevada, expressing his disillusionment with Malta’s political landscape and his desire to challenge the American Dream and capitalist culture. He believes that the US is at the root of climate change and that tackling it is crucial. Diacono was arrested alongside other climate activists during the protest and called for improvements in Burning Man’s environmental practices.

Moving on to North Dakota, the state heavily relies on oil money for its tax revenue, funding various sectors such as education and infrastructure. However, the state is facing challenges from the environmental, social, and governance (ESG) movement, which is impacting federal energy regulations and the economy. To combat this, experts suggest that North Dakota could lead in enhanced oil recovery and carbon management. Projects like Project Tundra and Summit Carbon Solutions are crucial in countering the opposition to the energy industry. Unfortunately, the Public Service Commission recently denied the permit for Summit Carbon Solutions' CO2 pipeline project, despite 80% of landowners supporting it. Overcoming opposition and supporting industries like oil, gas, coal, and ethanol is essential for North Dakota’s growth.

In the world of finance, experts at the Carbon & ESG Strategies conference highlighted the potential of carbon credits to reduce greenhouse gases and make money. However, they emphasized the need for standardization to create a free market approach. Many companies outside the oil and gas industry are interested in buying carbon credits to reduce their carbon footprint. Standardization is crucial for robust trading, and regulations are being developed to ensure compliance. Distributed ledger technology and blockchain can provide transparency and assurance in meeting carbon credit market fundamentals. The panelists believe that free market forces can drive solutions for the climate transition.

In New Zealand, KiwiSaver funds invested in companies causing harm have reached a record high of $8.6 billion, according to charity Mindful Money. While some funds are avoiding harmful companies, many are still investing in them. Fund managers argue that they can influence these companies through engagement and proxy voting. They cite examples of providing feedback to companies, resulting in apologies and changes in behavior. While the influence of KiwiSaver funds is limited for global firms, they can have a significant impact on New Zealand companies.

Finally, the article discusses the emerging trend of shareholder derivative lawsuits in Europe that aim to force corporate commitment to emissions reduction and sustainability. While these types of lawsuits have not gained traction in the United States, there has been a surge of shareholder derivative actions focused on issues such as sexual misconduct and diversity and inclusion. The article suggests that corporate lawyers should encourage proactive steps to mitigate the risks of potential future litigation and judicially-mandated corporate reform. It also highlights the importance of sustainability in business practices and the need for companies to view sustainability as a business opportunity. The article provides recommendations for companies to address sustainability in their corporate strategies and board discussions, monitor public statements and disclosures, consider shareholder demands, and recognize the importance of sustainability accountability.

In conclusion, these news summaries highlight the ongoing debates and actions surrounding climate change, sustainability, and corporate responsibility. It is important for individuals and companies to stay informed and engaged in these discussions to create a better future for our planet.
