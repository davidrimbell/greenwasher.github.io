S&P Global Drops ESG Scores Amid Backlash, Highlighting Challenges in Sustainability Reporting

Published: 2023-08-15

In recent news, Standard & Poor (S&P) Global has made a significant decision that will impact how companies are assessed for their environmental, social, and governance (ESG) performance. S&P Global Ratings had been using ESG scores to rate corporate borrowers since 2021, but they have now decided to drop this metric due to customer backlash and confusion surrounding its association with “woke” agendas. While S&P Global has made this change, other rating agencies like Moody’s and Fitch continue to use ESG assessments.

This move by S&P Global highlights the ongoing debate and challenges surrounding sustainability reporting and greenwashing. Greenwashing refers to the deceptive practice of making misleading or unsubstantiated claims about the environmental benefits of a product, service, or company. It is a form of deception, misrepresentation, and dishonesty that can mislead consumers and investors.

To address these concerns, the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) has issued a Public Statement outlining its expectations for sustainability-related disclosures in prospectuses. ESMA recognizes the importance of sustainability to investors and aims to promote transparency and consistency in sustainability reporting. The statement emphasizes the inclusion of material sustainability-related disclosures and the integration of non-financial reporting into equity prospectuses. This move by ESMA aims to minimize inconsistent standards and enhance transparency for investors.

However, it is not just regulatory bodies that are working to combat greenwashing. Secretive organizations that blacklist and censor conservative news outlets online are also facing criticism for their anti-free speech efforts and bias. These organizations, such as NewsGuard and the Global Alliance for Responsible Media, are accused of trying to enforce approved narratives and punish disapproved ones. House Judiciary Committee Chairman Jim Jordan has even subpoenaed officials from these organizations over potential antitrust law violations.

Amidst these controversies, there are companies that are genuinely committed to sustainability and making a positive impact. Energy Systems Group (ESG) has been selected as one of the 21 Energy Services Companies (ESCO) to be awarded a place on the Department of Energy’s Generation 4 Federal energy savings performance contract (ESPC) indefinite delivery indefinite quantity (IDIQ) contract list. ESG specializes in sustainable energy solutions and offers services such as energy efficiency, sustainability, resiliency, and infrastructure improvement. Their inclusion on this list highlights their dedication to providing sustainable infrastructure solutions and contributing to a greener future.

Another company making strides in sustainability is Iberdrola, which has launched a new carbon credit firm called Carbon2Nature. This firm focuses on nature-based carbon capture solutions and aims to implement projects in Latin American countries and the Northern Hemisphere. Carbon2Nature’s projects will be certified against internationally recognized quality standards, providing a more dependable option for companies looking to reach their net-zero goals. This initiative reflects Iberdrola’s commitment to reducing global carbon emissions and improving biodiversity while maintaining profitability.

In conclusion, the world of sustainability and greenwashing is complex and ever-evolving. While some companies and organizations are taking steps to address greenwashing and promote transparency, others are facing criticism for their biases and anti-free speech efforts. It is important for consumers and investors to stay informed and critically evaluate the claims made by companies. By supporting genuinely sustainable initiatives and holding companies accountable for their actions, we can work towards a greener and more sustainable future for generations to come.
