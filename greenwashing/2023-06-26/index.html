New Rules and Standards Aim to Combat Greenwashing and Improve Sustainability Reporting

Published: 2023-06-26

The fight against corporate greenwashing is gaining momentum as new rules and standards are being proposed to combat deception, misrepresentation, and fraud in sustainability claims. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) has released its inaugural standards, which aim to regulate companies' green claims and improve trust in climate reporting.

These rules would require firms to disclose their impact on the climate, including Scope 3 emissions. The goal is to establish a global language for climate reporting, inform investors about sustainability risks and opportunities, and ultimately create a more transparent and accountable business environment.

Several countries, including the UK, are considering implementing these standards to crack down on greenwashing. The London Stock Exchange and the Financial Conduct Authority have welcomed the new rules, recognizing the importance of accurate and reliable sustainability reporting.

The G20 has also backed new global rules that will put pressure on companies to disclose how climate change affects their business. These rules, published by the ISSB, build on voluntary ones from the G20’s Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD). Countries like Canada, Britain, Japan, and Singapore are considering adopting these standards for annual reports from 2024 onwards.

However, the fight against greenwashing is not without its challenges. The term “ESG” (environment, social, and corporate governance) has been misused and twisted by political extremists, leading BlackRock CEO Larry Fink to distance himself from it. Despite this, Fink remains committed to conscientious capitalism and the principles behind ESG investing.

In addition to regulatory efforts, companies themselves must also play a crucial role in combating greenwashing. PwC, a leading professional services firm, emphasizes the importance of companies using the new standards to produce high-quality sustainability reports. This means going beyond mere compliance and truly embracing transparency and accountability.

The need for sustainability extends beyond the corporate world. The demand for critical minerals, such as nickel, lithium, cobalt, and copper, for green energy technologies poses a threat to indigenous territories. Mining in these areas not only threatens indigenous rights but also the environment. Responsible mining practices and international standards are necessary to ensure the protection of human rights and the environment.

On a positive note, innovative solutions are emerging to help organizations measure and manage their carbon footprint and assess climate-related risks and opportunities. Sphera, for example, has launched its SpheraCloud Corporate Sustainability–Portfolio Management platform, which uses analytics and AI to collect, calculate, report, and manage financed emissions for financial institutions. This software aligns with industry standards and allows institutions to assess the emissions footprint of their investment and lending activities.

In conclusion, the fight against greenwashing is gaining traction with the introduction of new rules and standards. These efforts aim to improve transparency, accountability, and trust in sustainability reporting. However, it is essential for both regulators and companies to work together to ensure the effective implementation of these measures. By doing so, we can move closer to a more sustainable and responsible future for our planet and future generations.
