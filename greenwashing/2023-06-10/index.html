Controversy Surrounds Appointment of UAE Minister as President of COP Climate Conference

Published: 2023-06-10

The upcoming COP climate conference, set to be hosted by the United Arab Emirates (UAE), is already facing controversy. The appointment of Sultan Al Jaber, the UAE’s Minister of Industry and CEO of the National Oil Company (ADNOC), as the conference’s president has raised concerns. Greenpeace and other organizations argue that his position represents a conflict of interest and undermines the legitimacy of the conference. Al Jaber, however, has received support from other countries and has emphasized his commitment to decarbonization and renewable energy.

The involvement of the oil sector in COP decisions is seen by some as positive, as the UAE is actively transitioning away from fossil fuels. However, critics argue that the most polluting companies should be excluded from participating in the negotiations. The presence of fossil fuel interests at COP conferences has been a longstanding concern, and NGOs are calling for a protocol to exclude the fossil fuel industry from climate negotiations.

Banks are increasingly taking environmental, social, and governance factors into account when making financing decisions. They are aligning with principles such as the Equator Principles and the UN Global Compact. However, more needs to be done to achieve the United Nations' Sustainable Development Goals.

In Ghana, GCB Bank is contributing to the fight against climate change by implementing the “GCB Greens Ghana for Life” initiative. This initiative involves planting 7,000 trees and showcases the bank’s commitment to corporate responsibility. GCB Bank aims to make a real difference in the lives of Ghanaians and sees itself as a partner in sustainability.

Kohl’s, a popular retail store, is facing criticism and calls for a boycott. The store is reportedly reducing prices on its Pride collection after receiving backlash for selling “Pride” onesies for infants. Kohl’s is one of several retailers promoting LGBTQ+ inclusivity as part of its broader environmental, social, and governance (ESG) plan. The store has not responded to inquiries about whether the discounted prices indicate a lack of interest from shoppers.

Sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs) are seen as the key to reducing the carbon footprint of flying. However, their availability is currently limited. Airlines are looking to increase the supply of SAFs to meet their interim target of 30 billion liters per year by 2030. Synthetic fuels or e-fuels, which draw carbon dioxide from the air, are considered the most credible option. However, the feedstock required for SAF production presents practical and ethical challenges. The cost of developing SAFs is also a significant barrier. Some experts are skeptical about the scalability and sustainability of SAFs, suggesting they may function more like offsets. Airlines are also focusing on improving fuel efficiency through technological advancements.

The concept of fiduciary duty in relation to climate change is evolving. Fiduciaries now face a dilemma of prioritizing immediate profitability or addressing climate risks for long-term sustainability. The lack of tangible processes to assess and act upon climate-related risks is evident. Disclosure frameworks need to meet climate science, and litigation is influencing climate ambition and governance. Fiduciaries should consider climate risks to the extent that they intersect with financial interests. Fiduciary duty should evolve to legally recognize and integrate climate risks in investment frameworks.

As concerned individuals, it is important for us to stay informed about the various aspects of greenwashing, deception, and misrepresentation in the pursuit of sustainability. By understanding these issues, we can make more informed choices and hold companies and organizations accountable for their actions. Together, we can work towards a greener and more sustainable future for ourselves and future generations.
